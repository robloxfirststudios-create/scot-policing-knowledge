# Criminal Damage (Scotland)

Status: Draft — not legal advice.

This page is a draft summary intended for Police Scotland officers, staff and trainees. It is not legal advice. Always consult primary legislation, Crown Office & Procurator Fiscal Service (COPFS) guidance, and your force legal advisers for authoritative information.

Overview

- This page explains how incidents involving damage to property may be recorded, investigated, and progressed in Scotland. It is written as a policing reference and deliberately avoids attempting to give definitive legal statements.

Key points (high level)

- "Criminal damage" as a phrase is commonly used to describe deliberate or reckless damage to property. The precise legal definitions and statutory framework may differ between Scotland and other parts of the UK.
- In Scotland, prosecutorial decisions (including whether a reported incident results in prosecution) are a matter for COPFS. Police should gather evidence and present a comprehensive report.

Recording and initial response

- Ensure the scene is safe and preserve evidence.
- Record details: time, location, property affected, owner/occupier details, witness details, CCTV or dashcam evidence, and any suspect descriptions.
- Take photographs and, where appropriate, seize items for forensic examination following force procedures.
- Consider victim support and safeguarding needs.

Investigation checklist (suggested)

- Obtain and secure statements from victims and witnesses.
- Identify and secure forensic opportunities (fingerprints, DNA, toolmarks, CCTV, digital evidence).
- Check local CCTV, doorbell cameras, and nearby camera networks.
- Conduct neighbourhood enquiries and review intelligence systems for similar incidents or suspect behaviour.
- Use person and property checks (PNC/PND where applicable) to identify suspects or previous offending patterns.

Powers, arrest and detention

- Follow Police Scotland policy and statutory powers when considering arrest and detention. Whether an arrest is appropriate will depend on the facts, risks, and available evidence.
- If arresting, ensure custody records and evidential preservation steps are completed.

Charging and prosecution

- COPFS has exclusive responsibility for prosecution decisions in Scotland. The role of the police is to investigate and submit a report (the COPFS Report) with exhibits and statements.
- Include clear, chronological evidence and indicate any lines of enquiry that remain outstanding.

Sentencing and outcomes

- Sentencing is a matter for the courts. Possible outcomes include no action, fixed penalty/administrative disposal (where applicable), fiscal fines, or prosecution leading to court outcomes. Do not rely on this page for sentencing guidance.
